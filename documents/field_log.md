Based on "Delving into LLM-assisted Writing in Biomedical Publications Through Excess Vocabulary", we identify `excess words`. The LLM shift is characterized by `style-affecting verbs and adjectives`.

**Motivation**. The study was done 2 years ago. What has changed since then? Has there been a rebounce due to increased awareness to the LLM-ese markers and a real worry that one's text might be branded as LLM?

The "Word Spike" method (Kobak et al.) has a weakness: Authors can learn to avoid the words. If a researcher knows that "delve" is a giveaway, they will just delete it. It is much harder for a human to notice and fix their "Syntactic Fingerprint" (the average length of their dependency trees or their reliance on sentence-starting adverbs). By using spaCy, your not-an-llm tool is looking at the "skeleton" of the writing, which is much harder to hide than the "skin" (vocabulary).

**Milestones**. The idea is that there is feedback loop from research and the press. Analysis of the LLM-like writing makes researchers aware of the telltale signs of LLM generated-text, regardless of whether they use LLMs at all, or whether they use them for shameless slop, spell- and grammar-checks, or machine translation into the non-native English language.

## Feature Normalization Strategy

All features must be **scale-invariant**: a 50-page paper and a 5-page paper should be comparable on writing *character*, not just raw counts.

### Raw vs. Normalized Features

| Feature | Raw | Normalized | Normalization | Interpretation |
|---------|-----|-----------|----------------|-----------------|
| `clause_depth` | Max tree depth (raw) | `clause_depth_per_sentence` | ÷ (sentence_count + 1) | Average maximum depth; controls for document length |
| `dependency_entropy` | Shannon entropy of dep types | `dependency_entropy_normalized` | ÷ log(# unique dep types) | Entropy ∈ [0,1]; controls for how many syntactic types exist |
| `coordination_count` | Raw count | `coordination_count_per_1k_words` | ÷ (word_count / 1000) | Coordinating conjunctions per 1000 words |
| `sentence_depth_std` | Std dev (absolute) | `sentence_depth_cv` | ÷ mean_depth | Coefficient of variation; relative variability independent of baseline complexity |
| `list_of_three` | Raw count | `list_of_three_per_1k_words` | ÷ (word_count / 1000) | Parallel structure count per 1000 words |
| `dependency_length` | Already normalized ✓ | `dependency_length_norm` | Per-sentence average | Both are scale-invariant |
| `coordination_density` | Already normalized ✓ | — | ÷ (sentence_count + 1) | Count per sentence |

### Why Normalize?

1. **Cross-paper comparison**: A 10-page abstract and a 100-page thesis should be compared on style, not volume.
2. **Trend analysis**: Year-over-year changes in raw counts reflect both *writing style* AND *paper length trends* (which may change independently).
3. **Statistical rigor**: Normalized metrics avoid spurious correlations with document size.

### Exclusion from Plots

Raw aggregate features (`marker_words_total`, `marker_verbs_total`, etc.) and document metadata (`paper_count`, `sentence_count`, `word_count`) are excluded from generic trend plots to focus on *style* signals.
